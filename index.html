Qualifying Trading Volume (USD)†
Qualifying Trading Volume (USD)

The same qualifying trading volume as above, but expressed in US dollars: the total face value of all the positions traded.

This is notional turnover, not money deposited and not profit. It looks enormous (billions) for two reasons: trading uses leverage, so a small deposit can control a much larger position, and the same money is traded over and over.

A simple way to picture it: if you trade the same $1,000 ten times, that is $10,000 of “turnover” even though only $1,000 ever existed, and leverage stretches it further still. So read this as a measure of trading activity, not cash.

This month only. About $3.80 billion in April and $3.12 billion in May. See the note in the Data Quality section (†).

Gross Deposit
Gross Deposit

The total amount of money paid in (deposited) during the month, added up across every participant, before any withdrawals are subtracted. It is the gross, or raw, inflow of cash.

Unlike FTD Amount, this is not limited to first deposits. It counts every deposit made in the month: first-time deposits, repeat top-ups, and deposits from clients who had funded long before, all of it, from new and existing clients alike. Think of it as everything that came in the front door, no matter who sent it or what later went back out.

That is why it dwarfs FTD Amount. In April, first-ever deposits were only $6,465, yet Gross Deposit was $673,336, so the vast bulk of the money in was top-ups and deposits from people who had already funded before, not brand-new depositors.

It is measured before withdrawals. Net Deposit (the next row) takes this same money and subtracts what clients pulled back out, which is why Net comes out much smaller.

This month only. $673,336 in April and $777,437 in May each cover just that month’s deposits, not a running total.

Net Deposit
Net Deposit

Money paid in minus money withdrawn during the month. It is what actually stayed with TMGM once clients’ withdrawals are netted off, the real net change in client funds for the month.

It is simply Gross Deposit minus withdrawals. Gross is everything that came in the front door; Net subtracts everything that went back out the same month, leaving what genuinely remained.

Withdrawals were heavy, so the two sit far apart. In April, $673,336 came in but about $508,248 was pulled back out (roughly 75%), leaving just $165,088 net. May was similar: $777,437 in, about $577,079 out, leaving $200,358.

So Net is the more realistic measure of what the business actually held onto from clients. Some of these withdrawals came from clients taking funds out soon after reaching the deposit level needed for a gift, which the redemption_review flags in the master trace and the Findings section covers.

This month only. $165,088 in April and $200,358 in May each cover just that month, not a running total.

These 24 people reached a gift level, claimed their gift, and then withdrew money afterwards. Because the rules count net deposit as total deposits minus total withdrawals, taking money back out lowers their net deposit, and for these 24 it drops below the level the gift was for.

Is that allowed? Withdrawing your own money is not banned, and on its own it can be completely innocent. The catch is that the gift is meant to reward money genuinely kept in and traded, so once the net deposit falls below the level, the person no longer truly meets what the gift was for. The concern is the deliberate version: topping up just enough to qualify, claiming the gift, then pulling the money straight back out.

The Terms and Conditions are written for exactly this. Clause 4(b) says net deposit is total deposits minus total withdrawals, so a withdrawal directly reduces it. Clause 4(l) lets TMGM reject the gift and deduct its value ($15 for Tier A, $35 for Tier B, $70 for Tier C) from anyone judged to be exploiting a loophole. Clause 9 goes further, letting TMGM deduct all gifts from anyone it reasonably believes has taken unfair advantage. So withdrawing is not automatically against the rules, but this is the pattern those clauses exist to catch, which is why these 24 are flagged for a closer look rather than judged on the spot.

About the trading volume figures. "Qualifying Trading Volume" is the total worth of all the buying and selling a person did, added up. It is not the money they put in.

Picture someone at a market with $100 in their pocket. They buy a box of goods, sell it, use that money to buy another box, sell it again, and keep going all day. By the end they might have bought and sold thousands of dollars' worth of goods, even though they never had more than $100 to start with. This figure counts everything that changed hands, not the cash they began with.

Trading adds one more twist. A person can place trades much bigger than the cash they actually have, a little like putting down a small deposit to take home something far more expensive. So the totals build up very quickly and can reach into the millions and billions, even though the money actually deposited is much smaller.

So this number shows how much trading happened, not how much money is sitting in accounts. A large share of it here came from just a few very active traders.
